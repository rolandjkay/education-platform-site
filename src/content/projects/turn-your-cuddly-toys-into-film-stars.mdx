Do you dream of making films, but you have no one to film? Maybe you have no siblings, or you have siblings but they'd rather play video games than act for you. Of course, you could act yourself, but when you're trying to create beautiful images, it can be a lot of work having to be in front of the camera, as well as behind. If you have some cuddly toys in the house — or maybe some Lego figures or toy vehicles — you can still take some beautiful shots. Who knows? When your acting friends see how beautiful you can make a stuffed bear look, they will be begging you to have them act in your movies!

## The importance of production design

You want your shots to look cinematic, right? But you're filming them in your bedroom and, unless you are very lucky, your bedroom doesn't look especially cinematic. So, you need to make your bedroom look like a Hollywood movie set. Sounds expensive? Well, in fact, you can do a lot for very little.

The thing is, you only need to make things look good from the camera's point of view. If your scene is taking place in a restaurant, there is no need to build a swanky restaurant in your bedroom — you can place some objects in your scene to suggest a restaurant without having to build the whole thing.

## Think about depth

One thing that makes a shot look attractive, and more like a real scene, is depth. Make sure to include foreground and background elements so that we believe that your characters exist within a 3D space. You can simplify by thinking in layers: place your characters in the centre of the room, build a background layer behind them, and a foreground layer between them and the camera.

This photo shows how we did this. We put some black cloth at the back to hide the living room curtains and then hung some paper lanterns and fairy lights for an attractive background. For the foreground, we hung some plastic ivy in front of the camera. We arranged Scabbers and Lavender on a small table in the middle of the room between these two layers.

The camera can't tell that there are only three layers, and so with this simple setup we've successfully hidden the living room and created a convincing scene.

## Keep the foreground and background out of focus

Now, we want the audience to believe that Scabbers and Lavender are in a swanky members' club — but swanky clubs aren't usually decorated with old paper lanterns and cheap plastic ivy. We don't want our low-budget props giving the game away!

The trick is to keep as much of the foreground and background elements out of focus as possible. If we can keep them blurry while our characters are sharp, this draws the audience's attention to Lavender and also helps to hide the cheapness of our decorations.

The lights are the best example of this. We used the cheapest set of fairy lights you could find, and they'd already been used a bunch of times, so they were kind of mangled. But, just so long as they light up, out of focus lights look gorgeous, no matter how cheap they are.

If you are using a digital camera, achieve this out-of-focus effect by setting the aperture as wide as possible — if you see numbers like f1.8, f2, f4, choose the lowest number. We used f1.4. This is easier the larger your camera; phones traditionally struggle with this, though some modern phones have AI assistants that detect background elements and blur them for you.

## Fog and haze

We love to see texture in images, and if an image is too clean and precise it can feel artificial. In a real club there would be dust in the air, steam from the kitchens, humidity. We often don't notice these things, but we sometimes notice their absence.

One way to add texture is fog. Fog and haze make a scene feel much more real.

> ⚠️ **Warning:** Do NOT set fire to anything — not even paper — in an attempt to make smoke. Real smoke is bad for your health and you risk starting a fire.
>
> ⚠️ **Warning:** Do NOT try to use steam either. It doesn't hang in the air the way you want, and boiling large quantities of water is dangerous!

Fortunately, you can buy a cheap 500W fog machine for £30–£40. You're not going to be fogging up an outdoor location with this little machine, but it's sufficient to fill your living room.

## Move the camera

When looking at a 2D picture, your eyes are much better at working out where things are in 3D space if things are moving. We can increase the feeling of depth by moving the camera.

We chose to move the camera down to reveal our characters from behind the foliage. The fog was hanging in stratified layers, and these layers are much more visible as the camera moves through them, which adds even more texture to the image.

Some ideas if your camera doesn't have built-in stabilisation:

- Hold the camera on a skateboard or rollerskate and roll it along a table top.
- Hold the camera tight against your chest and rock your body — plant your feet so you're well grounded.
- Fold up a heavy tripod with the camera still attached and hold it at the balance point.

## Lights!

Besides what you choose to put in your scene, the second most important thing is lighting. Proper lighting can really make your shots look professional, and you don't need anything complicated or expensive.

- **Paper lanterns:** use electric tea lights — add enough to get the brightness you need. ⚠️ Do NOT use candles; you will set fire to the lanterns!
- **Fairy lights:** cheap and gorgeous when out of focus in the background.
- **RGB strip lights:** if you already have some, they're fun to experiment with.
- **Main lights:** we used two Neewer TL96 RGB lights — one backlighting the characters and picking out the haze, one illuminating the foliage and providing fill on the characters. You could substitute any household lamp; try to use a daylight bulb if possible and diffuse the light through tissue paper or a muslin square to make it softer.
