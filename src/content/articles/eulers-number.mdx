Most people have heard of $\pi$ — even if only as "that 3.14-something number" from school. Fewer people have heard of $\pi$'s lesser-known cousin $e$.

$$
e = 2.71828...
$$

$e$ shows up in the formula for how populations grow, how radioactive atoms decay, and how bank accounts compound. Just like $\pi$, it isn't something mathematicians invented — it's a property of the universe, and you can determine its value at home.

$e$ is a little harder to get your head around than $\pi$, so if you haven't already read [our article on $\pi$](/articles/pi), it's worth a look first — the ideas are similar, but $\pi$ gives you a gentler warm-up.

## When growth feeds on growth

Imagine a single bacterium in a dish. Every hour it splits in two. After one hour there are 2; after two hours, 4; after three, 8.

| Hour ($t$) | Bacteria | Added this hour |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| 0 | 1 | — |
| 1 | 2 | 1 |
| 2 | 4 | 2 |
| 3 | 8 | 4 |
| 4 | 16 | 8 |
| 5 | 32 | 16 |
| 6 | 64 | 32 |
| 7 | 128 | 64 |
| 10 | 1024 | 512 |

Look at the "Added this hour" column. At every row, the number of new bacteria equals the number already there. The bigger the colony, the faster it grows — not just a little faster, but proportionally faster: double the colony, double the growth rate.

> **What does "proportional" mean?**
> Two quantities are proportional if one is always a fixed multiple of the other. Your speed is proportional to throttle position if doubling the throttle doubles the speed. Here, the growth rate of the colony is proportional to the size: double the bacteria, double the growth rate.

<BacteriaGrowthSimulation />


This isn't unique to bacteria. The same pattern — *rate of change proportional to current size* — shows up everywhere:

- **Epidemics**: each infected person passes the infection to several others. The more people are infected, the faster the infection spreads. This is why cases during the early stages of an epidemic — like Covid-19 in early 2020 — double in a fixed number of days.
- **Radioactive decay**: each unstable atom has a fixed probability of decaying per second. The more atoms are present, the more decays happen. The population falls faster the bigger it is — proportional decay.
- **Compound interest**: the larger your account balance, the more interest you earn each year. Your wealth grows at a rate proportional to itself.

All of these follow the same underlying mathematical pattern, and so it would be useful to have a formula that can describe any process where the rate of growth is equal to the number of things there are.

OK, so we have our key insight. Now let's set up the maths to describe it.

## “Continuous” functions

The number of bacteria doesn't change continuously, that is to say, smoothly over time. Instead, one minute there are 32 and the next 64.
Mathematically, though, it's easier to analyse continuously changing functions. We can still use 
continuous functions for our population of bacteria, if we just agree to only look at the value of the function every second,
because saying there are 4.3345 bacteria doesn't mean anything.


## The $a^t$ family

Let's call the number of bacteria that we have $n$.

The bacteria that split in two follow $n = 2^t$. Bacteria that split in three would follow $n = 3^t$. 
Any process where the rate of change is proportional to the current value can be written as:

$$
n = a^t
$$

for some value $a$ that describes the speed of the process.

Now here's the key question. We said the growth rate of the bacteria is proportional to their number. What's the fixed multiplier that connects the two?

> **Quick note:** The "growth rate" is how fast the population is changing at a given moment — estimated as the change in population divided by the time elapsed, over a tiny slice of time. The "constant of proportionality" $k$ is the fixed multiplier that connects the growth rate to the current population size: $\text{growth rate} = k \times n$. When we plot a function, the growth rate is equal to the slope of the curve.

Looking at the table above, it looks like it should be 1.0, right? The change in the number of bacteria is equal to the number that we already
had. The fact is, though, that that is only true from one generation to the next. We want a continuous function that gives the correct number of bacteria in each 
generation. We can see in the graph below that the slope of this function will be less than 
$1.0 \times n$, and so $k < 1.0$.

<ExponentialSlopeGraph />

Let's estimate the fixed multiplier for $n = 2^t$. 

At $t = 3$ there are $2^3 = 8$ bacteria. 

A short time later, at $t = 3.01$, there are $2^{3.01} \approx 8.056$.

> Saying we have 8.056 bacteria doesn't make any sense, but we don't 
> mind because we agreed to only consider the value to represent the actual number of bacteria every second.

The growth rate over that tiny interval is:

$$
\frac{\text{change in population size}}{\text{time passed}} = \frac{8.056 - 8.000}{0.01} \approx 5.55
$$

The value at $t = 3$ is 8 and the growth rate is about 5.55 — roughly 69% of the value.

Now try $t = 7$:

There are $2^7 = 128$ bacteria. At $t = 7.01$, roughly $2^{7.01} \approx 128.89$. The growth rate over that interval is:

$$
\frac{128.89 - 128.00}{0.01} \approx 89
$$

That's again about 69% of 128.

The proportion is constant: for $n = 2^t$, the slope is always about 69% of the value, wherever you look, and so, for $n = 2^t$

$$
k \approx 0.69
$$

**What about $n = 3^t$?**

At $t = 1$, $n = 3$. At $t = 1.01$, $3^{1.01} \approx 3.033$. The growth rate over that interval is:

$$
\frac{3.033 - 3.000}{0.01} \approx 3.3
$$

That's about 110% of the value. For $n = 3^t$, $k \approx 1.10$.

- $a = 2$: $k \approx 0.69$ — slope is 69% of value
- $a = 3$: $k \approx 1.10$ — slope is 110% of value

$k$ is less than 1 when $a = 2$ and greater than 1 when $a = 3$. Since $k$ changes continuously as $a$ changes, there must be some value of $a$ between 2 and 3 where $k = 1$ exactly: where the slope equals the value at every point on the curve.

We're going to call the value of $a$ at which $k = 1$ by the name $e$ — just as we gave the name [$\pi$](/articles/pi) to the area of a circle of radius 1.0 in our [article on $\pi$](/articles/pi).

So we know $e$ is somewhere between 2 and 3. Let's find it.

## Estimating $e$ — Expanding on our bacteria example

> The next few paragraphs show *why* the formula works — feel free to skip ahead to the table if you're happy to take it on trust.

Remember we talked about continuous functions above. We want to find a continuous function that doubles every second, so that it models 
our bacteria population. We already know that doubling every second means our constant of proportionality $k=0.69$. That means 
that the growth rate of our population is $kn(t)$.

> $n(t)$ just means the size of the population at time $t$. So, $n(t+10)$ is the size ten seconds later than $t$.

Let's divide each second up into 
$m$ equal parts. That's the same as saying each part lasts for $d = 1/m$ seconds; when $m=2$ each part lasts 0.5 seconds; when $m=10$ each part lasts
$0.1$ seconds, and so on.

If the population is growing at a rate of $k \times n(t)$ per second then the population at $t+d$ will be $n(t) \times k \times d$ larger. 
In other words, the increase in the population is equal to the growth rate $\times$ time passed.
We can write this as

$$ 
n(t + d) =  n(t) \times (1 + k\times d ) 
$$

and, since $d = 1/m$

$$ 
n\left(t + d\right) =  n(t) \times \left(1 + \frac{k}{m} \right) 
$$

To see how the population grows from $t$ to $t+d$, we multiply by $(1+k/m)$. If we want to see how it grows from $t$ to $t+2d$, we need to 
multiply by $(1+k/m)$ twice, once to go from $t$ to $t+d$ and a second time to go from $t+d$ to $t+2d$. 

If we want to go from $t$ to $t$ + one second, we need to multiply by $(1+k/m)$ $m$ times, because there are $m$ parts in one second.

$$ 
n(t + 1) = n(t) \times \left(1 + \frac{k}{m} \right)^m
$$

So, we now have a way of giving the function $n(t)$  multiple values between the times  of $1.0$ seconds, $2.0$, $3.0$ while respecting two important considerations

1) We don't change the value of $n(t)$ at $t=1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0$, so this equation still models the bacteria population
2) The growth rate is equal to $k \times n(t)$ at all the intermediate values.

We can make $m$ as large as we like and the larger we make it the more continuous $n(t)$ will be. We can see that if we make it very large indeed 
then we will have a continuous function. In other words,

$$
    n(t + 1) = n(t) \times e^{k} \approx  n(t) \times \left(1 + \frac{k}{m}\right)^{m} 
$$

And so

$$
   e^{k} \approx \left(1 + \frac{k}{m}\right)^{m} 
$$

This equation works for any value of $k$, so let us choose $k=1$.


$$
   e \approx \left(1 + \frac{1}{m}\right)^{m} 
$$


In plain English: if you split each second into $m$ tiny intervals and let the population grow a little bit in each one, the total growth after one second gets closer and closer to $e$ as you make the intervals smaller.

We can get an estimate of the value of $e$ by choosing a large value of $m$ and using the above equation. The larger we make $m$, the better our estimate will be.

For example, with $m = 2$:

$$
\left(1 + \frac{1}{2}\right)^2 = 2.250
$$

And with $m = 4$:

$$
\left(1 + \frac{1}{4}\right)^4 \approx 2.441
$$

Let's plot our estimate of $e$ for larger and larger values of $m$

## $e$ is everywhere

Once you know what $e$ is, you see it everywhere.

Any quantity that grows or decays at a continuous rate proportional to its own size follows the same pattern. Don't be put off by the notation — this just says that the amount after time $t$ is the starting amount multiplied by a growth factor that depends on $e$:

$$
A = A_0 \cdot e^{rt}
$$

where $A_0$ is the starting amount, $r$ is the growth rate, and $t$ is time. This single formula covers:

- **Populations** growing at a constant continuous rate
- **Radioactive decay** (with $r$ negative)
- **Compound interest** in the continuous limit
- **The cooling of a hot cup of tea** — the temperature drops at a rate proportional to how far above room temperature it is (Newton's law of cooling)

The reason $e$ keeps appearing is exactly the property we found: for $y = e^t$, slope equals value everywhere. 
Any process driven by "rate proportional to current size" can de expressed using $e$.


## Over to you

Next time you see exponential growth mentioned — a virus spreading, a viral video going viral, a technology improving at pace — you now know what's really happening underneath. Each thing that exists is spawning more things, which spawn more things still. The whole process is governed by a number no one invented: $e = 2.71828...$

Just like $\pi$, it was always there, waiting to be found.
