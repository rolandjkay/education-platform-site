It sounds like a trick question. A hundred pounds is a hundred pounds, right? Well, not quite. And once you understand why, you've got your hands on one of the most powerful ideas in all of finance.

## Money now is worth more than money later

Here's the simplest reason: if someone gives you £100 today, you can put it in a savings account and earn interest. In a year's time, you'll have more than £100. So £100 now is genuinely worth *more* than £100 in a year — not because of inflation (though that matters too), but because of what you can *do* with it in the meantime.

This idea has a name: the **time value of money**. It is the foundation of almost every decision in finance — from whether to take out a mortgage, to how a company decides whether to build a new factory, to how a pension fund values your retirement savings.

## Compound interest: money that makes money

Before we can go further, we need to talk about compound interest. You've probably come across simple interest before — you put £100 in a bank at 5% per year, and you get £5 at the end of the year. Easy.

But compound interest is different, and far more powerful. With compound interest, the interest you earn also earns interest. Here's how it plays out:

| Year | Amount at start | Interest earned (5%) | Amount at end |
|------|----------------|----------------------|---------------|
| 1    | £100.00        | £5.00                | £105.00       |
| 2    | £105.00        | £5.25                | £110.25       |
| 3    | £110.25        | £5.51                | £115.76       |
| 4    | £115.76        | £5.79                | £121.55       |
| 5    | £121.55        | £6.08                | £127.63       |

By year 5, you've earned £27.63 — not the £25 you'd get from simple interest. It doesn't sound like much, but over decades, compounding becomes extraordinary. Einstein is often (probably apocryphally) quoted as calling compound interest "the eighth wonder of the world". Probably apocryphal, but the sentiment is right.

## The formula

We can write all of this down cleanly. If you invest a sum of money today (called the **present value**, or PV) at an annual interest rate $r$, then after $n$ years you'll have a **future value** (FV) of:

$$
FV = PV \times (1 + r)^n
$$

Let's check it against our table. After 5 years at 5%:

$$
FV = 100 \times (1.05)^5 = 100 \times 1.2763 = £127.63
$$

Spot on.

## Running it backwards: present value

Now here's where things get really interesting. We can run that formula in reverse. If someone promises to pay you £127.63 in five years' time, and the going interest rate is 5%, what is that promise worth to you *today*?

We just rearrange the formula:

$$
PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^n}
$$

$$
PV = \frac{127.63}{(1.05)^5} = \frac{127.63}{1.2763} = £100
$$

Which makes sense — that £127.63 in five years is worth exactly £100 today, because £100 today would grow to £127.63 at 5%.

This process of working backwards — taking a future sum and finding what it's worth today — is called **discounting**. The interest rate used is called the **discount rate**.

## Why this matters in the real world

This isn't just a maths exercise. The time value of money shows up everywhere:

**Mortgages.** When you take out a mortgage, the bank lends you money now and you pay it back in monthly instalments over 25 years. The bank uses present value calculations to work out how much interest to charge so that all those future payments add up to the right amount today.

**Investing.** When you buy a share in a company, you're essentially buying a claim on all its future profits. Investors use discounting to figure out what all those future profits are worth today — and whether the share price is good value or not.

**Should a company build a new factory?** A factory costs money now but generates profits in the future. By discounting all the future profits back to today and comparing with the upfront cost, a company can decide whether the investment is worth making. This is called **Net Present Value (NPV)** analysis, and it's how most major investment decisions are made.

**Pensions.** If you want £30,000 per year in retirement starting in 30 years, how much do you need to save today? The time value of money tells you.

## The rule of 72

Here's a handy trick. If you want to know roughly how many years it takes for money to double at a given interest rate, just divide 72 by the interest rate:

$$
\text{Years to double} \approx \frac{72}{r\%}
$$

At 6% interest, your money doubles in about 12 years. At 9%, it doubles in 8 years. At 1% (a typical savings rate for much of the 2010s), it takes 72 years — longer than most people's working lives. Which is one reason why sensible investing matters.

## The flip side: inflation

We said earlier that inflation matters too. It does, and it works in the same direction as the time value of money. If inflation is running at 3% per year, then £100 in five years buys less than £100 today. Real-world discount rates often include both the return you could earn *and* the inflation you're trying to beat.

## Over to you

The time value of money is one of those ideas that, once you see it, you see it everywhere. Next time someone offers you a payment plan — buy now, pay later, zero interest for 12 months — you'll know exactly how to think about it. That £500 laptop on a payment plan isn't really free credit; the retailer has done the maths, and so should you.
