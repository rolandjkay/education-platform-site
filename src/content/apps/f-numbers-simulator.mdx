The **Light Falling on Sensor** bar (bottom-right) gives a quick visual read of the current exposure level. The **graph** beside it shows the full curve — how exposure falls off as the aperture-sensor distance increases. The white dot marks your current position on that curve.

## Background

### The f-number

A camera lens focuses light from the scene onto the sensor. The key variable you control in this simulator is the distance between the aperture (the opening in the lens) and the sensor — this is the **focal length**, $f$. The diameter of the aperture opening is $D$.

The **f-number** is simply their ratio:

$$
N = \frac{f}{D}
$$

A small f-number means a wide aperture relative to the focal length; a large f-number means a narrow one. But why does this ratio matter so much for exposure? The answer lies in some simple geometry.

### How much light reaches the sensor?

Every ray of light that passes through the aperture continues travelling outward and intersects an imaginary **hemisphere** centred at the aperture, with radius equal to the focal length $f$. You can see this hemisphere in the simulator — toggle it with the *Hide/Show Image Hemisphere* button.

The surface area of that hemisphere is:

$$
A_\text{hemisphere} = 2\pi f^2
$$

The sensor sits at the back of this hemisphere and has a fixed area $A_\text{sensor}$. It only captures the fraction of the hemisphere it covers:

$$
\text{fraction captured} = \frac{A_\text{sensor}}{2\pi f^2}
$$

Meanwhile, the total light entering through the aperture is proportional to the aperture's area, which scales with $D^2$. Putting these together, the light actually reaching the sensor is:

$$
E \propto D^2 \times \frac{A_\text{sensor}}{f^2}
$$

### Expressing this in terms of the f-number

Since $N = f/D$, we have $D = f/N$. Substituting:

$$
E \propto \frac{f^2}{N^2} \times \frac{A_\text{sensor}}{f^2} = \frac{A_\text{sensor}}{N^2}
$$

The $f^2$ terms cancel. The sensor area is fixed, so we are left with a beautifully simple result:

$$
\boxed{E \propto \frac{1}{N^2}}
$$

The exposure is inversely proportional to the square of the f-number — and nothing else. Focal length and sensor area drop out entirely.

### F-stops

Photographers work in **stops**, where each stop is a doubling or halving of the light. Since $E \propto 1/N^2$, halving the exposure requires multiplying $N$ by $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41$. This gives the familiar f-stop series:

$$
f/1 \;\to\; f/1.4 \;\to\; f/2 \;\to\; f/2.8 \;\to\; f/4 \;\to\; f/5.6 \;\to\; f/8 \;\to\; f/11 \;\to\; f/16
$$

Each step multiplies $N$ by $\sqrt{2}$, which squares to 2, halving the light. Move two stops and you quarter the light; three stops cuts it to one eighth — and so on.

The **Fraction of light hitting sensor** readout in the simulator counts exactly these stops from the widest aperture and displays the result as $1,\ \tfrac{1}{2},\ \tfrac{1}{4},\ \tfrac{1}{8},\ldots$
