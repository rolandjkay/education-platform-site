Explore how changing the distance between the aperture and the sensor of a camera affects which parts of a scene are in focus, and how much light reaches the sensor.

## Interactive controls

**Blue arrow (on the image plane)** — Drag it left or right to change the distance between the aperture and the sensor. Moving it closer shrinks the effective focal length; further away increases it.

**Object distance arrow** — Drag to move the subject closer to or further from the camera.

## Top bar buttons

| Button | What it does |
|---|---|
| Guided Tour | Flies the camera to each key component with a short annotation |
| Full Screen | Expands the app to fill the browser window |
| Hide / Show Camera Body | Toggles the camera body mesh so you can see inside |
| Hide / Show Tube | Toggles the lens barrel |
| Hide / Show Image Hemisphere | Toggles the hemisphere that visualises the field of view |

## Status bar (bottom)

| Readout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Aperture | The f-number — ratio of focal length to aperture diameter |
| ISO | Equivalent sensor sensitivity needed to maintain exposure |
| Fraction of light hitting sensor | How much of the available light actually reaches the sensor, relative to the shortest focal length |

## Light meter and graph

The **Light Falling on Sensor** bar (bottom-right) gives a quick visual read of the current exposure level. The **graph** beside it shows the full curve — how exposure falls off as the aperture-sensor distance increases. The white dot marks your current position on that curve.

## Background

The **f-number** (or f-stop) is the ratio of the lens focal length to the diameter of the entrance pupil:

**N = f / D**

A low f-number (e.g. f/1.4) means a wide aperture, letting in lots of light but producing a shallow depth of field. A high f-number (e.g. f/16) gives a deep depth of field but requires more light or a longer exposure.
